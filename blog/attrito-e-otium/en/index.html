Otium & memory · 22 July 2026 · 9 min read

Where there is friction, something remains

We live in a time that has turned speed into a virtue and pausing into a fault. We run, not always because we have a destination, but because stopping would force us to ask where we are going. We fill every interval, occupy every silence, measure our days by what we have produced, solved, delivered. Even rest has to be useful, scheduled, high-performing.

Memory, however, obeys a different law.

We do not remember everything we pass through. We mostly remember what we collided with: the people who contradicted us, the experiences that disoriented us, the words that found an exposed part of us. We remember what did not slide away without resistance.

Where there is friction with the world, there is memory.

Friction is not just conflict. It is the point where reality stops being a backdrop and becomes presence: the rub between what we wanted and what happened, between the image we had of someone and what that person turned out to be, between the plan and the unexpected direction life took.

A perfectly smooth surface leaves no marks. It holds nothing, changes nothing. What resists, on the other hand, forces us to slow down, shift posture, rethink our stride. The obstacle, the disappointment, love, loss, even misunderstanding become forms of knowledge because they force us out of autopilot.

Every deep emotion contains a resistance. True joy interrupts the ordinary course of time. Grief weighs it down. Love upends priorities. Fear shrinks the world, nostalgia reopens it. Even serenity, when it is real, is not the absence of movement: it is a balance reached after crossing a tension.

Emotions are not decorations on our existence. They are inner frictions: the way reality meets our sensibility and forces it to take shape.

People, too, enter our lives through friction. Some welcome us, others challenge us. Some encounters produce warmth, like two surfaces touching and recognising each other; others leave wounds. But the decisive encounters almost never leave us unchanged. A true relationship is not free of friction: it is able not to turn it into destruction right away.

The problem of our time is not that there is too much friction. It is that we no longer know how to stay inside it.

Every discomfort must be removed, every wait shortened, every void filled. At the slightest unease we reach for a distraction, a notification, a piece of content. We no longer give experiences time to settle: we consume them before understanding them. So we accumulate events and lose meaning.

The frenzy does not come only from too much work. It comes from the difficulty of inhabiting time when time produces nothing visible. We have learned to feel legitimate only while busy: being busy has become the way we confirm to ourselves that we are necessary. But a life that is completely occupied risks never being truly lived.

This is where the Latin ōtĭum becomes useful again.

Today "idleness" sounds like an accusation. For the Romans it held much more: time free from public duties, rest from business, private life; stillness, calm, even truce. Otium was not the opposite of work: it was the opposite of total absorption in urgencies and obligations. A space carved out of the noise, in which to return to oneself. Not necessarily to do nothing, but to do what cannot be measured by immediate usefulness: thinking, reading, remembering, conversing, observing. The time in which life settles into consciousness.

We have almost abolished that space. Even when we are not working we remain in the negotium: the body stops, the mind keeps negotiating with the world. We check messages, anticipate problems, replay conversations. We have free time, but rarely freed time.

The distinction is essential. Free time can be just a gap between two commitments. Freed time does not have to justify itself: it does not have to be productive, interesting or shareable. It does not have to become content. It can stay silent.

In that silence, though, friction makes itself felt again. And perhaps that is exactly why we avoid it.

When we stop, the postponed questions resurface, the unprocessed emotions, the people we lost, the decisions we lacked the courage to make. Speed protects us from listening. Constant busyness can become an elegant form of escape.

Otium, then, is not a comfort: it is an act of courage. It means ceasing, for a moment, to anaesthetise our friction with the world, and recognising that some experiences do not ask for a solution but for a presence.

Not everything should be gotten over quickly; some things must be crossed slowly. Grief is not solved: it changes shape. Nostalgia is not eliminated: you learn to listen to it. Disappointments sometimes hold a truth we did not want to see, and even anger can point to the exact spot where a deep value was violated. Without a space of stillness, though, all of this remains noise.

Otium turns noise into language. It is in the pause that we understand why a sentence hurt us. It is in the distance that we recognise a person's worth. It is in the calm that experiences stop being mere events and become consciousness.

Memory itself needs slowness. Remembering is not storing images: it is giving them a place inside us. A memory is not what stays identical, but what keeps being reinterpreted in the light of who we have become. That is why friction generates memory: because it changes us. Memory is born at the point where the world no longer matches our expectations.

Not every friction, however, has to become a wound. Friction produces heat, but also movement: without it we could not walk, the foot would slip without advancing. Perhaps the same goes for existence. Resistances are what let us move forward, as long as they do not become so intense that they immobilise us.

Maturity, perhaps, lies in telling apart the frictions that consume us from those that transform us. There are people who hurt us without teaching us anything, and others who throw us into crisis because they see a possibility we cannot see yet. There are jobs that tire us and jobs that empty us. Silences that protect and silences that punish. Truces that heal and idleness that hides the fear of starting again.

Authentic otium is not permanent stillness: it is the pause that gives direction back to movement. A truce, not a surrender.

Creativity needs room

Creativity, too, is often born in this apparently unproductive territory.

We like to tell it as a flash of inspiration, a gift falling from the sky onto a privileged few. But creativity is more like a muscle: it trains, tires, regenerates and, above all, feeds on experience. We become creative by reading, listening, observing, building, taking apart, getting it wrong. By playing with an idea without knowing where it will lead.

The creative gesture looks spontaneous, but behind it there is almost always a long sedimentation: images, conversations, attempts and failures that the mind kept combining while it seemed busy with something else. It is born neither from discipline alone nor from talent alone: it is born where training meets freedom.

Which is why it cannot be ordered like an administrative task. It does not show up because we sat down at the desk and decided it was time to produce. It appears in the shower, on a walk, while driving or cooking. Not because those activities are wasted time, but because part of the mental work continues even when we stop supervising it.

Hence the most common misunderstanding: believing that productivity is proportional to the hours spent materially performing an activity. Eight hours in front of a screen are not eight hours of useful thought. One hour of genuine focus, preceded by a walk or by an apparently empty afternoon, can produce more than a whole day spent forcing the mind.

There is a time for execution and a time for incubation: the moment when we write and build, and the one in which we gather materials without yet knowing how we will use them, let a problem rest, wait for a solution to ripen away from conscious attention.

Not doing, then, is part of doing. A field farmed without pause does not become more fertile: it needs rest, empty seasons. The same goes for a producing mind. Subjected non-stop to goals, notifications and micro-decisions, it can keep executing but gradually loses the ability to imagine: it becomes efficient at repeating and less and less capable of deviating.

Creativity, instead, is almost always born from a deviation. From an unplanned connection, from a mistake that opens a different road, from an activity done not because necessary but because enjoyable. Play is not the opposite of work: it is one of its best laboratories. We keep doing what amuses us; what we keep doing becomes skill; skill, when it meets the freedom to experiment, generates new possibilities.

We should stop treating as suspicious everything that does not produce an immediate result. A conversation, a trip, a film, a video game, a book read with no professional purpose can become, weeks or years later, the invisible material an idea will be born from. We cannot always predict the value of what we are living while we are living it.

An exhausted mind can still complete tasks. It will hardly invent worlds.

Defending the truce

Perhaps we need a contemporary form of otium: not hours of isolation, but spaces in which we stop reacting automatically.

A walk with no destination. A conversation with no phone on the table. An afternoon without the duty to document it. The time to stay in front of a question without immediately hunting for the answer. The ability to get bored long enough to feel an authentic desire resurface.

Small gestures, almost subversive. In a society that profits from our fragmented attention, guarding stillness means defending a part of ourselves. Before what we do, there is someone who feels, interprets, imagines and remembers.

The centre is not a place we reach once and for all: it is something we must return to. We lose it every time the outside noise drowns the inner voice; we find it again when we interrupt the race long enough to recognise what is happening to us.

The real opposition of our time, perhaps, is not between work and rest: it is between dispersion and presence. One can be motionless and completely dispersed; one can be active and deeply present. Otium is the possibility of not being elsewhere.

The world, in any case, will keep producing friction. People will disappoint and surprise us, experiences will contradict our plans, some emotions will pass through us without asking permission. We cannot make life smooth, nor would it be desirable. But we can decide not to consume every experience in the rush to get to the next one: to stay long enough to understand the mark it left, to turn friction into awareness, impact into memory, pause into direction.

Personally, I aim at a freedom that is very simple to state and very hard to win: never feeling guilty when I'm doing fuck all or thinking about fuck all.

Because, honestly, I have already done plenty. And I will keep doing plenty more.

The to-do list will always be full: every completed item spawns two more. A mind that likes to produce never truly reaches the end of its list, because it keeps seeing possibilities, problems to solve, roads to take. Waiting to finish everything before allowing yourself a truce means condemning yourself to never rest.

That is why I want to learn not to treat emptiness as a fault. Not to measure my days only by what tangible thing they leave behind. To recognise that, sometimes, thinking about nothing is exactly what thought needs.

Not all apparently empty time is wasted time. Sometimes it is the place where experiences settle, emotions find a name and ideas get ready to exist. Other times it serves not even that. And that is fine too.

We must not turn rest itself into a technique for producing more, nor justify every truce by promising we will work better afterwards. We have the right to stop even when the pause produces nothing, solves nothing and does not make us better people.

The most authentic otium begins right here: when we stop valuing time only by what we extract from it.

Because where there is friction with the world, there is memory. But only where a truce still exists can that memory become meaning. And only when we learn to do nothing without feeling guilty can we go back to doing something because we truly want to, not because we are afraid to stop.
