Expertise & method · 19 July 2026 · 5 min read

The cobbler was right

Apelles was the most famous painter of antiquity, and he had an odd habit. He would show his panels in the town square, then hide behind them to eavesdrop on what people said. One day a shoemaker stopped and told him a figure's sandal was wrong: an eyelet was missing. Apelles said nothing. That night, quietly, he fixed the detail.

The next day the shoemaker came back, saw the change and grew bolder. He started criticizing the leg. That is when the painter stepped out and put him in his place with a line that became proverbial: "Cobbler, not beyond the shoe."

We usually remember only this second part. The retort that shuts up the meddler, the praise of staying in your own field. It is the easy reading, the one we reach for to dismiss anyone who wanders in where they don't belong.

The part we forget

But there is a first part, and it matters more. About the sandal, the shoemaker was right. He knew nothing about art, about proportion, about light and shade. He knew shoes. And because he knew shoes, he caught a mistake that had slipped past the greatest painter alive. Apelles did not argue and did not pull rank: he looked, he checked, he fixed it.

So before it is a lesson in manners, the anecdote is a small lesson in method. The scientific method lives right in the tension between the two halves of the story. On one side you take correction from anyone who can actually verify a piece of the problem, title or no title. On the other you distrust authority the moment it reaches past what it can put to the test, however impressive it is elsewhere.

Knowledge is not measured in diplomas but in range. The shoemaker is reliable on the sole and useless on the calf; the painter rules the body but cannot see the stitching. Neither of them is "the expert" in any absolute sense. Each is one inside a boundary. And serious work starts precisely by working out where that boundary runs.

Anyone who builds meets him daily

If you build things, you run into the shoemaker constantly. He is the user who writes "this button does nothing." He has no idea how the software is put together, he will never read a line of your code, and yet he has just spotted a flaw you could not see because you were standing too close. The urge to reply "you don't understand how it works" is enormous, and it is almost always the wrong instinct. On the sole, he is right.

The reverse holds too. It is just as easy to become the Apelles the shoemaker sees: to feel good at one thing and talk yourself into being good at everything, to stretch your authority from the sandal to the leg, from your own trade to the whole world. Method is the discipline that keeps those two apart. Take the right observation seriously, and know when to stop, in others and in yourself, a moment before the calf.

You don't have to pick one face of the story. You need both, in the right order: first you listen, on the ground where the other can check; then you weigh things up, clearly, when that ground runs out. It is an awkward stance, because it asks for humility and firmness at once. But it is the only one that holds the painter and the shoemaker together. And with them, nearly everything worth building well.
